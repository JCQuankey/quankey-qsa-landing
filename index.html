The problem in practice

NIST has set 2035 as the deadline to disallow RSA, DH, and classical ECC (NIST IR 8547, November 2024). NSA CNSA 2.0 requires PQC in network protocols by 2030. Before you can plan a migration, you need to know where you stand today.

  • Inventories are incomplete. Endpoints sprawl across cloud, on-prem, and partner connections. Most teams cannot produce a current list of what cipher suites and key exchange methods are actually negotiated in production.
  • Configurations drift. Certificate rotations, vendor updates, and infrastructure changes silently reintroduce weak defaults. A configuration that was strong last quarter may not be strong today.
  • Audit evidence is manual and non-repeatable. Point-in-time checks with browser tools or ad-hoc scripts produce results that are hard to compare, hard to version, and hard to defend in a review.

What QSA does — and does not do

QSA focuses on TLS perimeter assessment. The scope is deliberately narrow to keep results actionable.

In scope

  • Scan host:port targets from your environment
  • Capture negotiated TLS parameters: protocol version, cipher suite, key exchange group (including PQC hybrid signals)
  • Classify crypto hygiene and PQC readiness; produce machine-readable JSON output and a self-contained HTML report
  • Diff successive scans: improved, degraded, or neutral — with reasons per endpoint

Not in scope

  • Not a TLS proxy, VPN, or WAF — QSA is not inline with traffic
  • Not a PQC migration service — QSA measures readiness; it does not deploy or configure PQC algorithms
  • Not SaaS — runs on your infrastructure with no telemetry by default

Why it matters

If you don't measure, you can't prioritize

Without a baseline scan, remediation planning and PQC roadmaps rely on assumptions. Teams debate priorities without data. QSA gives you the inventory to make those calls with evidence.

If you don't track, drift goes unnoticed

Configuration changes, certificate rotations, and vendor updates can silently downgrade your posture. Periodic scans with drift diffs make regressions visible before they become audit findings.

If you can't repeat, audits are expensive

Manual checks are slow, subjective, and hard to compare across time or across teams. Repeatable scans with hash-chain integrity turn posture assessment into a routine operation, not a project.

Our approach

On-prem by design

Runs as a static Go binary (~10 MB) or Docker container on your network. No data leaves your environment. Suited for air-gapped and critical infrastructure environments.

Vendor-neutral posture view

Scans any TLS endpoint regardless of the underlying stack. Mixed environments — cloud, on-prem, legacy — get a single consistent assessment with the same scoring methodology.

Evidence-first reporting

JSON output with hash-chain integrity (SHA-256). Each evidence pack is reproducible, verifiable, and structured for auditor consumption and compliance review.

Drift diffs for change control

Compare scans to track what changed, in which direction, and why it matters. Built for change advisory boards, periodic review cycles, and continuous posture monitoring.

How it works

Three commands cover the workflow: scan, diff, and verify.

# 1. Scan endpoints from a target list
qsa scan --targets endpoints.txt --output ./results

# 2. Compare scans to track drift
qsa diff baseline.json current.json

# 3. Verify evidence pack integrity
qsa verify evidence-pack.json
